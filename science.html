The Science of Stress

The feeling of being stresses is familiar to many of us. Almost every single one of us is stressed at some level, from the moment we wake up, until we go to bed. However, our stress perception doesn’t always reflect accurately how stressed we are at a biological level and if we are properly recovering after a stressful situation. Therefore, in addition to psychological evaluation of stress (i.e. stress questionnaires), objectifying stress by measuring different biological molecules, enable the understanding of this complex process.

What is stress?

Stress is part of the human condition and our body is designed to experience stress and react to it. Stress is defined as a normal reaction to a change that requires adaptation or response. Our autonomic nervous system has a built-in stress response that allow the body to combat stressful situations, the so called “flight or fight” reaction, which is activate in case of an “emergency”.

Stress can be positive, stimulating, keeping us alert and ready to avoid danger. But if the person faces continuous challenges without relief or relaxation, the body’s recovery capacity is exceeded, the stress-related tensions will be build up. Thus, this repeated/chronic stress will become negative and will then manifest in a variety of ways. Some would feel mostly tensed and nervous, others more anxious, and/or depressed, other will feel excessively tired, exhausted, over emotional, losing focus while some others will have physical manifestations as pain, having a skin rash etc. Moreover, the perception of stress differs greatly among people and can be affected by, among others, the individual’s beliefs, attitudes and occupation.

How does stress impact our health?

When we perceive a situation as ‘dangerous’ or ‘threatening’, our body switch into the fight or flight state, an survival instinct developed since the beginning of the humanity. In our modern world, we no longer experience the stress of hunting for food, living in cold or being hunted by other creatures. However, our bodies have now the same biological reactions as our ancestors had ten of thousands years ago. Thus, when we encounter a stress, our blood pressure rises, our breath will accelerate, we begin to perspire and our blood become sticky in case we would have an injury. If this states is repeated too often or almost constantly, as is the case of a prolonged stress, the body will start to suffer at emotional and/or physical levels.

We all know how bad stress can be for us.

Therefore, chronic stress will conduct to several hormonal imbalances, especially cortisol (the “stress hormone”), but also sex-hormones like oestrogen and testosterone, insulin, melatonin could be also disturbed. Thus symptoms like fatigue, nervousness, weight gain or difficulty to lose weight (especially the belly fat), low lobido, sleep problems etc can occur as a single symptom or as serie of complaints.

Moreover, several messenger molecules and neurotransmitters secreted in the brain and nervous system can be affected by stress: serotonin (the happiness molecule), dopamine (involved in motivation and reward loup), epinephrine (involved in performance and stress process), norepinephrine (involved in mental alertness) etc. Consequently, this myriad of imbalances, all together or separately, can conduct to a variety of psycho-neurological symptoms like anxiety, depression, foggy mind, loss of memory, sugar cravings.

In addition, it showed that stress can cause unhealthy damaging behavioural changes, engaging people in the compulsive use of substances to try to relieve stress like unhealthy eating, increased drinking and smoking or the usage of drugs, gambling and other addictions.

Stress can be also linked to physical symptoms like migrena, painful periods for women, eczema, irritable bowel syndrome etc. Many researches show that stress can trigger or worsen many diseases- depression, burn out, obesity, cardio-vascular, auto-immune diseases, aging, cancer to mention only few of them. What’s more, stress can interfere with our circadian rhythm, which is essential for our health and wellbeing.

How stress is measured?

One of the best indicator that someone is under stress is a change in the amount of cortisol produced by their body throughout the day- the cortisol rhythm test. Thus, cortisol has a circadian secretory rhythm, with a peak in the morning, being at highest in the morning, shortly after getting up. It then quickly drops in the early afternoon and then will slowly decreased in the late evening, preparing the body to the inactivity and relaxations prior to a night sleep. During the night, especially the second half, the cortisol will increase again and will be at his highest peak again in the morning. However, in case of chronic stress, this rhythm can become chaotic or as a reversed curve (low in the morning and during the day and high at night). Therefore, the deviations of the level of cortisol from their normal course are a good indicator of the current stress load.

In acute stress situations, the morning-time outpouring of cortisol increases and normalises in the course of the day. In the case of continuous stress, the entire daily curve shifts upward, meaning that the cortisol level is elevated permanently. If the stress load remains and turns into chronic stress, the daily rhythm may become "chaotic", meaning that the level of cortisol moves outside of the normal values for the time of day. If the chronic stress persists even longer, then, at some point, the production of cortisol ceases and cortisol level drops below the normal level. Lowered cortisol values are being measured in, for example cases of burnout syndrome.

Another test of importance is DHEA, which will counterbalance the cortisol effects, among others things. If both, DHEA and cortisol, both secreted by adrenal glands are low or limit low, there is a red flag for burn out.

Additionally, some molecules and neurotransmitters can give an indication about stress’ process:
Serotonin, Epinephrine, Norepinephrine, Dopamine, GABA, Glutamate
